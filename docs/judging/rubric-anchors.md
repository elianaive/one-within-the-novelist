# Rubric Anchors: 10 Resonance Dimensions

Full score-level descriptions for all 10 resonance dimensions. Judges receive
these anchors in their evaluation prompts. Each dimension has explicit
descriptions for scores 1 through 5.

The anchors are grounded in cognitive science research (cited per dimension) and
calibrated against Prometheus 2's rubric format, which achieves 0.897 Pearson
correlation with human evaluators when using criterion-specific per-score
descriptions.

These are the **shared baseline dimensions** used across all stages. Individual
stages may add stage-specific dimensions (e.g., Stage 1 adds "generative
fertility"; Stage 4 adds "voice adherence") but cannot remove or redefine these
10.

---

## 1. Transportation / Immersion

*Does the story pull the reader into its world?*

Grounded in Green & Brock's transportation theory (2000): full transportation
requires three simultaneous components — cognitive absorption (focused attention),
affective involvement (emotional engagement), and vivid imagery (mental
simulation of scenes).

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **1** | No pull. The reader remains entirely outside the story. Prose is abstract, generic, or disjointed. No sensory detail, no emotional stakes, no reason to pay attention. Reading feels like processing information, not experiencing a world. |
| **2** | Intermittent engagement. Occasional moments of imagery or emotional connection, but the story keeps breaking the spell — through inconsistencies, exposition dumps, tone shifts, or lack of specificity. The reader dips in and out. |
| **3** | Sustained engagement with reservations. The reader enters the story world and stays for stretches. Imagery is present, emotional stakes are clear, attention is held. But something prevents total absorption — perhaps the prose calls attention to itself, or a false note disrupts the dream (Gardner's "vivid and continuous dream" is continuous but not fully vivid, or vivid but not fully continuous). |
| **4** | Strong transportation. The reader loses awareness of the reading act for extended periods. All three components active: cognitive absorption (thinking about the story's implications), affective involvement (feeling something), vivid imagery (seeing the scenes). Minor interruptions possible but the reader re-enters quickly. |
| **5** | Total immersion. The reader forgets they are reading. Time passes unnoticed. The story world feels more present than the physical environment. All three Green & Brock components operating at full intensity simultaneously. The state where a reader looks up from the page disoriented, needing a moment to remember where they are. |

## 2. Suspense Architecture

*Does the story create and manage tension effectively?*

Grounded in Brewer & Lichtenstein (1982): suspense arises from discourse
structure — uncertain outcomes for a protagonist at risk. Zillmann's excitation
transfer (1996): residual arousal from unresolved tension amplifies the emotional
peak at resolution.

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **1** | No tension. Events unfold without stakes or uncertainty. The reader has no reason to wonder what happens next. Outcomes feel predetermined or irrelevant. The story is inert. |
| **2** | Nominal tension. Stakes are stated but not felt — the reader knows they should care about the outcome but doesn't. Tension may be present in premise but dissipated by execution: too much foreshadowing, insufficient investment in characters, or stakes that feel artificial. |
| **3** | Functional tension. The story creates genuine uncertainty about at least one outcome, and the reader wants to know the resolution. Tension builds and releases in a recognizable pattern. The architecture works but feels conventional — the reader can sense the structural mechanics. |
| **4** | Effective tension management. Multiple tension threads operating simultaneously. Build-up creates genuine anticipation; release delivers satisfying payoff. Zillmann's excitation transfer is active — the resolution feels amplified by the preceding tension. The reader's pulse quickens at the right moments. Some subversion of expected tension patterns. |
| **5** | Masterful suspense architecture. Tension operates on multiple levels simultaneously (plot outcome, character decision, thematic resolution). The story manages the reader's arousal state with precision — knows when to tighten, when to release, when to let tension simmer. The peak moment delivers the full excitation transfer: residual tension from unresolved threads amplifies the climactic release. The reader physically cannot stop reading. |

## 3. Curiosity / Information Gaps

*Does the story make the reader want to know something specific?*

Grounded in Loewenstein's information-gap theory: curiosity arises from
awareness of a gap between current and desired knowledge. Distinct from suspense
(epistemic vs. teleological).

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **1** | No information gaps. Everything is stated, explained, or obvious. The reader has no questions. Nothing is withheld, implied, or mysterious. The story tells rather than invites. |
| **2** | Weak curiosity. Minor questions exist but the reader doesn't strongly care about the answers. Gaps feel accidental (confusion) rather than intentional (mystery). Or gaps are created but immediately resolved, preventing curiosity from building. |
| **3** | Active curiosity. The story creates at least one genuine information gap that the reader wants resolved. The "page-turner" quality is present in at least one dimension — the reader would continue reading to find out. The gap is intentional and the reader trusts it will be addressed. |
| **4** | Strong pull-forward. Multiple information gaps operating at different timescales — a micro-gap resolved within the scene, a macro-gap spanning the story. The reader is actively constructing hypotheses about what's hidden. New information raises new questions rather than simply answering old ones. The gap structure creates momentum. |
| **5** | Compulsive curiosity. The story creates gaps that the reader cannot leave unresolved. Information is disclosed at a rate that maximizes the gap between what's known and what's desired — each revelation widens rather than narrows the curiosity. The reader's model of the story is perpetually incomplete in ways that feel tantalizing rather than frustrating. At a scene break or chapter ending, stopping is genuinely difficult. |

## 4. Emotional Depth

*Does the story produce genuine feeling, not performed emotion?*

Grounded in the paradox of fiction (Radford 1975): readers feel genuine emotions
for characters they know don't exist. Broader than "character depth" — emotional
depth can come from a concept (Borges), a situation (Jackson), a voice
(Hemingway), or a character (O'Connor).

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **1** | Emotionally flat. The story names emotions without producing them. Characters "felt sad" or "was overwhelmed with joy" but the reader feels nothing. Emotion is performed — the text gestures at feeling without creating it. Or the story is so detached that no emotional entry point exists. |
| **2** | Surface emotion. The reader recognizes what emotion the story intends to produce but experiences it weakly or not at all. The emotional beats are predictable — happiness where happiness is expected, sadness where sadness is cued. No surprise in the feeling. Emotion arrives on schedule and departs without lingering. |
| **3** | Genuine feeling. The story produces at least one moment of real emotional response in the reader — a pang of recognition, a flash of dread, a surge of tenderness. The emotion isn't just signaled; it's experienced. But it may be intermittent or not sustained throughout the story. |
| **4** | Complex emotion. The story produces emotions that are layered, contradictory, or difficult to name. The reader feels more than one thing simultaneously — tenderness and horror, admiration and unease, grief and relief. The emotional experience has texture and specificity that goes beyond simple valence (good/bad). |
| **5** | Devastating emotional depth. The story alters the reader's emotional state in ways that persist after reading. The feeling is precise — not generic sadness but the specific ache of a particular kind of loss; not generic fear but the particular dread of a specific inevitability. The reader may need to pause, re-read, or sit with the feeling. The emotion feels discovered rather than constructed — as if the story revealed something true about human experience that the reader already knew but hadn't articulated. |

## 5. Emotional Arc Coherence

*Does the emotional trajectory feel earned?*

Grounded in Reagan et al. (EPJ Data Science, 2016): six core emotional arc
shapes across 1,327 stories. The key word is *earned* — each shift must feel
like a consequence of what preceded it.

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **1** | No discernible arc, or an arc that feels random. Emotional shifts happen without cause — characters are happy, then sad, then happy again with no connecting logic. Or the story maintains a single emotional note throughout with no development. The trajectory feels arbitrary. |
| **2** | Recognizable arc but unearned. The story follows a standard emotional trajectory (e.g., despair-to-hope) but the transitions aren't supported by events or character development. The reader can see where the arc is going but doesn't believe the journey. Emotional turns feel manipulative rather than organic. |
| **3** | Coherent arc with adequate support. The emotional trajectory follows a recognizable shape, and each shift is motivated by story events. The arc makes logical sense. But it may feel mechanical — the shifts arrive when the structure demands them rather than emerging naturally from accumulated narrative pressure. Competent but not surprising. |
| **4** | Well-crafted arc with earned transitions. Each emotional shift feels like a natural consequence of accumulated events, character choices, and thematic development. The reader doesn't notice the arc as structure — it feels like life unfolding. At least one transition surprises in retrospect (the shift was inevitable but the reader didn't see it coming). |
| **5** | An arc that redefines the reader's experience of the entire story. The emotional trajectory builds to a culmination that recontextualizes everything that came before — earlier moments that seemed one way now feel different. Every element contributed to this trajectory. The arc shape itself feels meaningful, not just the events it contains. Reagan's "man in a hole" or "Cinderella" shapes at their most powerful — not the template, but the feeling of inevitability that the template describes. |

## 6. Causal / Logical Coherence

*Do events follow from each other, or do they feel random?*

Grounded in Chen & Bornstein (Trends in Cognitive Sciences, 2024): causally
central events are rated more important and better recalled. For non-plot-driven
stories, "causal" extends to logical, implicational, or thematic coherence.

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **1** | Events feel random or disconnected. Things happen but nothing causes anything else. Characters act without motivation. The story could be rearranged without loss. No sense of necessity or connection between parts. |
| **2** | Weak connections. Some events follow from others, but the chain has gaps — characters make decisions that aren't supported by their established psychology, or events occur that require coincidence or contrivance. The reader has to work to connect the dots and sometimes can't. |
| **3** | Functional coherence. Events follow a logical chain. Character decisions are motivated. The reader can trace causation from beginning to end without confusion. But the chain may be too neat, too linear, or too predictable — events follow because they must, not because they reveal anything. |
| **4** | Rich causal web. Multiple causal threads interact — a character's early decision has consequences they didn't intend, a background detail becomes causally central. The causal structure creates the feeling that the story world operates by its own consistent logic. Some causal connections are subtle enough that the reader discovers them on reflection. |
| **5** | Inevitability. Every element is causally load-bearing. The story builds a causal structure so tight that the ending feels like the only possible outcome — not because it was predictable, but because every prior event contributed to it. For non-plot stories: every passage connects to every other through logic, theme, or implication. The structure has the density of a poem — nothing is arbitrary, everything earns its place. Removing any element would collapse the chain. |

## 7. Surprise + Post-dictability

*Are there genuine surprises that feel inevitable in retrospect?*

Grounded in Bissell et al. (WNU @ ACL 2025): six criteria for narrative
surprise — predictability (inverse), post-dictability (retrospective coherence),
initiatoriness, immutability violation, importance, and valence.

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **1** | Completely predictable. The reader knows what will happen from early in the story and is never proven wrong. Or: random events that surprise because they're arbitrary, not because they emerge from the story's logic. Neither prediction-subversion nor retrospective coherence. |
| **2** | Mild surprise. One or two moments where the story doesn't go exactly as expected, but the deviations are minor or feel like variations on a template rather than genuine subversions. The reader's model of the story is mostly confirmed. |
| **3** | Genuine surprise. At least one moment where the story takes an unexpected turn that the reader did not predict. The surprise is meaningful — it changes something about the reader's understanding. Post-dictability is present but partial — looking back, the surprise makes sense but wasn't the only possible outcome. |
| **4** | Effective surprise with strong post-dictability. The story's key surprise feels both unexpected AND, in retrospect, inevitable. Looking back, the reader can trace the setup — clues were planted, the groundwork was laid — but they didn't see it coming in the moment. The surprise recontextualizes earlier events. The "of course" moment. |
| **5** | The revelation that remakes the story. A surprise so perfectly constructed that it transforms the entire reading experience. Low predictability (the reader genuinely didn't see it coming) combined with maximum post-dictability (in retrospect, it was the ONLY possible outcome). Everything in the story was building to this — every detail was simultaneously itself and a clue. O'Connor's grandmother reaching out to the Misfit. Jackson's reveal. Chiang's final realization. The moment when the pattern snaps into focus and the reader sees the whole story differently. |

## 8. Ending Quality

*Does the ending land?*

Grounded in Kahneman's peak-end rule: endings disproportionately determine
overall story evaluation. A weak ending sinks an otherwise excellent story; a
powerful ending redeems a shaky middle.

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **1** | The ending fails. It's abrupt without intention, or trails off without resolution, or contradicts the story's logic. The reader feels cheated, confused, or indifferent. The ending undermines what came before rather than completing it. Or: the ending is a non-ending — the story simply stops. |
| **2** | Weak ending. The story concludes but without force. The resolution is too neat (everything works out), too vague (nothing is resolved), or too predictable (the only possible ending arrives exactly as expected). The reader finishes without feeling the story has earned its conclusion. |
| **3** | Adequate ending. The story's dominant tension is resolved. The ending is coherent with what preceded it and provides sufficient closure. It doesn't undermine the story. But it also doesn't elevate it — the ending is the expected conclusion, delivered competently. The reader nods rather than gasps. |
| **4** | Strong ending. The ending provides both emotional resolution and thematic resonance — it concludes the story's events AND speaks to what the story is *about*. The Gestalt "click" is present — the pattern is complete. The ending leaves the reader with a specific feeling that lingers. It may surprise or it may satisfy, but either way it lands. |
| **5** | An ending that is the story. The final moment is so precisely calibrated that it becomes the thing the reader remembers. It provides the peak emotional experience AND the sense of completion simultaneously (Kahneman's peak-end delivered in one stroke). Ambiguous endings can reach 5/5 if they provide interpretive richness rather than mere incompleteness — the ending opens rather than closes, but opens onto something meaningful. The reader rereads the ending. Then rereads the whole story. |

## 9. Flow / Pacing

*Does the story maintain momentum?*

Grounded in Thissen et al. (2018, 2021): flow — effortless processing, loss of
time awareness, intrinsic enjoyment — is the **strongest predictor of reading
pleasure**, more predictive than suspense, identification, or cognitive
involvement independently. Csikszentmihalyi's flow balance: challenge must match
reader skill.

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **1** | The story stalls or exhausts. Pacing is either so slow the reader's attention wanders (over-description, repetitive scenes, no forward motion) or so rushed the reader can't process what's happening (events pile up without breathing room). The reading experience is effortful in an unrewarding way. |
| **2** | Uneven pacing. The story has passages that flow well interspersed with sections that drag or rush. The reader's engagement oscillates — drawn in during good passages, checked out during bad ones. The momentum is present but unreliable. |
| **3** | Consistent pacing. The story maintains a steady rhythm. The reader's attention is held throughout without major disruptions. Event density is appropriate — neither too sparse nor too dense. The prose processes easily. But the pacing may be too uniform — no acceleration toward climax, no deliberate slowing for emotional weight. Competent but undynamic. |
| **4** | Dynamic pacing. The story modulates its rhythm deliberately — accelerating toward tension peaks, slowing for emotional resonance, using sentence length and event density as expressive tools. The reader feels the pacing as part of the experience. Csikszentmihalyi's flow state is achieved for extended stretches. Scene transitions feel natural. |
| **5** | Effortless momentum. The story achieves Thissen's full flow state — the reader loses awareness of time, processes the prose without effort, and experiences intrinsic enjoyment of the reading itself. Pacing is invisible because it's perfect — the reader never thinks about speed, only about what's happening. The story *moves* even in its slowest moments. Every paragraph earns the next. The reader reaches the end surprised it's over, wanting more — not because the story was too short, but because the reading experience itself was pleasurable. |

## 10. Memorability / Distinctiveness

*Would the reader recall this story days later?*

Grounded in the story superiority effect (Mar et al., N>33,000): stories are
remembered better than facts. Within stories, emotional peaks drive memory
(Leong et al., Nature Human Behaviour 2025). The Sui Generis score (Xu et al.,
PNAS 2025) measures plot uniqueness.

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **1** | Forgettable. The story blends into the mass of competent fiction. No image, moment, or idea persists. Asked about it a day later, the reader would struggle to distinguish it from similar stories. Nothing sticks. |
| **2** | Partially memorable. One element lingers — perhaps an evocative image, an interesting premise, or a well-turned phrase — but the story as a whole doesn't persist. The reader might remember the concept but not the experience. |
| **3** | Memorable. The reader would recall this story a week later and be able to describe what made it distinctive. At least one "indelible image" — a scene, moment, or line vivid and emotionally charged enough to persist in memory. The story has an identity that separates it from other fiction. |
| **4** | Strongly distinctive. The story occupies its own space in the reader's mental landscape. Multiple elements persist — images, feelings, the shape of the experience. The reader would recommend it to others and be able to articulate why. It's not just remembered; it's *the kind of thing you tell someone about*. |
| **5** | Haunting. The story changes something in the reader's permanent mental furniture. Days later, a real-world experience triggers a memory of the story — not as a reference, but as a way of understanding the world. The story has become a lens. Leong's finding manifested: the emotional peak has been encoded with enhanced neural fidelity. The reader may not remember every detail, but the *feeling* and the *insight* are indelible. The story joins the small collection of fiction that genuinely altered how the reader perceives something. |

## Usage Notes

### Stage-Specific Dimension Weighting

Not all dimensions are equally relevant to all stories or all stages. The 10
dimensions are the **baseline vocabulary**, not a mandatory checklist:

- **Stage 1 (Concept):** Evaluates *potential* for these dimensions, not
  achievement. A concept scores high on "suspense architecture" if the premise
  supports effective tension management, not because tension is already present.
- **Stage 3 (Voice):** Only dimensions 1, 9, and 10 are primary (transportation,
  flow, distinctiveness). Suspense and emotional arc depend on content, not voice.
- **Stage 4 (Prose):** All dimensions at play. This is where the full rubric
  operates.
- **Stage 5 (Refinement):** All 10 dimensions at full weight — the complete
  assessment.

### Dynamic Rubric Overlay

These 10 dimensions are the baseline. The dynamic rubric system (see
implementation-tier-b.md) generates 3-5 additional story-specific dimensions
per evaluation. The dynamic dimensions use the same 1-5 scale and anchor format
but are tailored to what each specific story is trying to do.

### Calibration

These anchors should be validated against HANNA (1,056 stories, 6 dimensions)
and LitBench (43,827 preference pairs). If the judge panel's dimension rankings
diverge significantly from human ground truth on these datasets, the anchors
need revision. See implementation-scoring.md for the calibration procedure.
