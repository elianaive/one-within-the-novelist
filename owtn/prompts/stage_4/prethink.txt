Before any prose is written, you do the thinking. The deliverable is `pre_think.md` in your sandbox — it already exists with a `## scene_id` heading for each scene; you fill the bodies.

**The plan lives in `pre_think.md`. Reasoning in your text reply does not land — it disappears when the loop continues.** Every plan-bearing sentence must arrive through `edit_file` (for incremental fills) or `write_file` (for whole-file rewrites). Treat your text reply as scratch — the place to think out loud, name a problem, plan the next move — and treat the tool call as the act of planning.

Sit with the work as a whole before you plan its parts.

The pull to start at scene one and write a paragraph for each section in order is strong; the planning that produces strong drafts begins before the keyboard. Read the concept end-to-end. Read the voice — not as a spec, as a sound; let it settle in your ear. Read the structural plan whole, the way you'd read a story's blurb-and-outline before reading the story itself: what is this manuscript, what does it want to do to a reader, where will the work be hardest. Notice which scenes you already know how to render and which feel uncertain. The uncertain ones are where the planning matters most.

Then plan, scene by scene, in whatever order serves the work — you may find that planning the climactic scene first clarifies what the earlier scenes need, or that planning the opening tells you what the climax is for. The pre_think file is for you to think *into*, not just to fill out.

For each scene, cover whichever of these the scene wants:

- What the scene is doing in the story — what the reader is supposed to come away from this scene having registered. Not what happens; what the scene is *for*.
- Which edge payloads from the structural plan this scene must enact. For each one, the *enactment hypothesis* — *how* the prose will dramatize it. Which moment lands the disclosure; which physical action carries the cause; which silence weighs the prohibition. The hypothesis is a craft commitment, not a checklist. If a payload's right enactment hasn't surfaced yet, say so and circle back.
- Which voice moves the scene calls for, drawing from the voice's positive constraints and characteristic moves. Not the whole spec — the moves that this scene specifically wants.
- Which motifs surface in this scene and in what mode (introduced, embodied, performed, agent, echoed, inverted). How the motif appears, not just that it does.
- The reader-state at scene exit (see below).
- Continuity facts inherited from prior scenes — what has been established that this scene must respect; what entities have been named; what has been disclosed and to whom.

The reader-state at scene exit is the load-bearing target the prose writes toward. Three orthogonal dimensions:

1. **Epistemic** — what the reader knows, suspects, holds open, cannot determine. The most powerful dimension; the reader's relationship to information is what drives narrative tension. Vocabulary: *Reader knows X happened but not why. Reader suspects Y but has no confirmation. Reader holds open whether Z is reliable.* Be specific — the question the reader is leaving this scene unable to put down.

2. **Affective quality** — the *kind* of unsettlement, implication, resonance, dissonance the prose engineers in the reader. Structural language, not named emotions. Vocabulary: *Reader is unsettled in a way they cannot articulate. Reader feels the wrongness before they can name it. Reader is implicated in the character's choice without consent.* Avoid the fast vocabulary of feeling — *sad, hopeful, moved, devastated.* The work of the prose is to engineer the conditions for feeling, not to name what the feeling is.

3. **Forward pull** — what unresolved question or tension carries the reader into the next scene. What work does this scene leave for the next one to pick up?

If the voice resists reader-satisfaction-oriented design — fragmented, late-Beckett, Sebald-flat, deliberately-difficult — the affective dimension may not apply, and the reader-state element can be suppressed or sketched. Use your judgment from what the voice is doing.

When every scene's plan is in `pre_think.md`, call `finalize_pre_think`. Re-read the file before finalizing: if a scene heading still has an empty body, the plan is incomplete — fill it via `edit_file` first. The system tracks whether tool calls have committed content; calling `finalize_pre_think` while the file is empty will flag the plan as scratch.
