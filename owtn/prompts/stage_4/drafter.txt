Write the prose.

The manuscript lives at `story.md` in your sandbox. It has been scaffolded with one `## scene_id` heading per scene, in topological order; your work is to fill the bodies. Your scene-level planning lives at `pre_think.md` in the same sandbox; you wrote it earlier. The concept, voice, and structural plan are in your system prompt above.

Before you write anything, sit with the work as a whole. The pull to start at scene one paragraph one is strong; the writing begins before any sentence does. Read the concept again. Read the voice — not as a spec, as a sound. Read the structural plan end-to-end so the shape of the manuscript is in your head before you commit to any of its parts. Read your `pre_think.md` for the per-scene plans you already laid down. Notice what already feels alive to you and what you don't yet know how to land. That second category is where the work is hardest, and where the writing is most likely to slip toward generic. Sit with the discomfort of not-yet-knowing those scenes. Don't reach for the writing tool yet.

Then choose how to approach this manuscript.

There is no single right order to write a story. Some stories demand chronological writing — the voice and the world accumulate scene by scene the way the reader will accumulate them, and any other order risks a manuscript that doesn't believe in its own time. Some stories come into focus when you write the climactic moment first and then write backward to the opening that earned it. Some are best written from the central image out — find the one moment that *has* to be there, write that, and let the surrounding scenes take their shape from it. Others come together outside-in — opening and ending first, the middle filled in once the frame holds. Read the structural plan and the voice and decide what this specific manuscript wants. The plan lists the scenes in one possible topological order; that is one ordering, not a prescription.

**The prose lives in `story.md`. Prose in your text reply does not land — it disappears when the loop continues.** Every sentence that becomes part of the manuscript must arrive through one of the writing tools below. If you draft a scene in your reply instead of writing it to the file, that scene does not exist. Treat your text reply as scratch — the place to think out loud, name a problem, plan the next move — and treat the tool call as the act of writing.

Three tools for getting prose into `story.md`:

- `write_file` for whole-scene drafts and substantial rewrites of a scene already in place — full-file overwrites of `story.md`. This is the tool you reach for to put a new scene's prose down. `path: "story.md"`, `content: <the entire updated manuscript including the scene you just wrote>`.
- `edit_file` for mechanical find/replace inside `story.md` once the prose is there: a phrase you want to swap, a sentence you want to retire. It does the substitution and nothing else.
- `dispatch_surgical_edit` is the strongest editing move you have. When a passage needs a reader to hold the surrounding prose in mind while reshaping just that part — a paragraph that wants rebuilding from the ground up, a beat where the dramatization isn't landing, a moment whose voice is off — dispatch a focused-passage edit. The dispatched reader sees the full manuscript and writes a replacement bounded to the passage you describe.

Aim for the strongest version of each scene you can land on this pass. Specific where it could be general. Voice-true sentence by sentence. Payloads enacted, not stated. Moments inhabited, not named. Don't get stuck recursively polishing one sentence — finish the scene; a sentence that isn't quite right yet reads more clearly when the surrounding prose exists. Forward motion is how the voice holds across length; getting trapped on word three of paragraph two is how it decays.

Pause between scenes. After a scene is on the page, sit with it before writing the next one. Use `think` to read what you've just written *as a reader*, not as its author. Notice where the voice landed and where it loosened, where a payload was enacted and where it slid past, where a moment was inhabited and where it was named. The reader-mode read is a different operation from the writer-mode write; the same words look different from each side. If a small fix is in reach, take it. If a bigger problem surfaces — the scene's weight is in the wrong place, the disclosure didn't land, the voice slipped late — record it in `note_to_self` for the revision pass and keep moving. The next scene needs the voice you are still in.

If a scene resists you — if the prose keeps coming out generic, if the voice slips on every attempt, if you've written the same opening sentence three different ways and none of them is the one — stop and sit. Use `think`. Re-read the bench passages cached at the top of your prompt; let the voice come back into your ear. Re-read the prior scene to remember what register the manuscript was just in. Then try again, with whatever angle you missed the first time. The model's default reasserts in the moments where the writing is hardest; the move is to slow down, not to push through.

When the manuscript has its scenes on the page, call `finalize_down_draft`. The first revision pass exists to do the deeper read; what you produce here is the strongest first version of each scene you can land, not a polished draft and not a placeholder.
