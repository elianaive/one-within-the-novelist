The scale-1 and scale-5 prose below each dimension describes the *endpoints of quality* on that dimension; they are not pointwise scores you assign. Use them as intuition for what "decisive" feels like (the gap spans nearly that full endpoint-to-endpoint range) versus "narrow" (both concepts sit in broadly similar territory with one edging the other). The full magnitude spec and output format live with the task in the user message.

1. NOVELTY
Does this concept see something that hasn't been seen this way before?

Sub-criteria — answer each before scoring:
(a) DOMAIN CROSSING: Does the concept combine domains, registers, or contexts not conventionally paired? Name the crossing. ("The Husband Stitch" = domestic memoir x body horror folklore. "Story of Your Life" = linguistics fieldwork x grief x deterministic physics.)
(b) CONVERGENCE DISTANCE: Does the premise avoid the 10 known AI convergence patterns (reconciliation arc, grief meditation, chosen one, AI consciousness, sanitized conflict, epistolary revelation, time loop, magical-realism-as-metaphor, moral clarity, small-town secret)? If it maps to one, does it subvert the pattern so it works against itself?
(c) GENERATIVE SURPRISE: If 5 different writers tackled this premise, would they produce noticeably different stories — or converge on the same plot? ("The Metamorphosis" yields horror, comedy, allegory, family drama. "A detective solves a murder" converges regardless of who writes it.)

1: Well-known trope, no subversion, single obvious execution. Five writers would produce the same story.
5: The premise is an invention. Reading it changes what the evaluator thinks is possible. Untraceable to existing patterns. Inexhaustible.

2. GRIP
Does this concept contain the engine to pull a reader in and not let go?

Sub-criteria:
(a) THE THING YOU CAN'T LOOK AWAY FROM: Does the premise seize attention? This can be a question the reader needs answered, but the strongest hooks are concretizations — something abstract made visible and undeniable. Not described, but embodied. ("The Metamorphosis": alienation given a body. "The Lottery": conformity given stones. "The Yellow Wallpaper": confinement given a pattern on a wall.) Name what seizes attention.
(b) EMOTIONAL STAKES: Is something specific at risk that a reader could care about? Name what's at stake — not "there are stakes" but the specific thing. ("A Good Man Is Hard to Find": a family's lives, but also the grandmother's need to believe she's a good person. "Sonny's Blues": whether a brother can actually hear the person his brother has become.)
(c) SENSORY SEED — the anchor scene: Read the concept's `anchor_scene.sketch`. Is this a visible scene or a gesture at one? A visible scene lets you see what is *in the frame* — the black box, the stones, the blind man's hand on the pen, the grandmother reaching out. A gesture describes the effect without the scene. (Jackson's villagers drawing slips. Carver's blind man guiding the narrator's hand on the pen. O'Connor's grandmother reaching out to touch the Misfit.)

1: Abstract, generic, inert. No image, no stakes, no reason to keep reading.
5: The concept produces a physical response from the premise alone. All three sub-criteria fire simultaneously. The reader cannot put it down.

3. TENSION ARCHITECTURE
Does the concept create inherent pull-forward force?

Sub-criteria:
(a) SUSPENSE: Is there an uncertain outcome for someone the reader might invest in? Name it. ("Where Are You Going, Where Have You Been?": will Connie go with Arnold Friend? "The Yellow Wallpaper": will the narrator escape or break?)
(b) INFORMATION ARCHITECTURE: Does the concept withhold information the reader would want? Name what's withheld — and distinguish between resolvable gaps (the story will eventually answer) and permanent gaps (the story structurally refuses to resolve). Permanent gaps are gravitational centers the reader orbits indefinitely. ("The Lottery": what is the lottery? Resolvable. "Bartleby": why does he prefer not to? Permanent. "Hills Like White Elephants": what exactly is being discussed? Permanent.)
(c) WEIGH-BACKWARD — the anchor scene: Read the `anchor_scene.sketch`. Does this scene commit the preceding material to retroactive meaning — does the story's opening read differently after this beat? Grounded in Aristotle's recognition/reversal pair and Baxter's rhyming action. The anchor applies one of three overlapping kinds of backward pressure: a `reveal` commits through **new information** disclosed; a `pivot` (from kishotenketsu's "ten") commits through **a new frame on existing information**; a `climax` commits through **surprising-yet-inevitable necessity** — making the ending feel necessary while also revealing what earlier scenes were actually about at character or theme level. Disclosure is itself a species of reframing, and climaxes routinely disclose; these are sources of retroactive pressure, not cleanly separate mechanisms. The test is the same for all: does the anchor weigh backward? ("Story of Your Life": memory is foreknowledge. "A Good Man Is Hard to Find": the grace moment reframes the grandmother's prior self-delusion as the thing that had to break.)

1: Static. No uncertainty, no withheld information, no room for recontextualization.
5: The concept IS tension. Multiple types feed each other. Permanent gaps create gravitational centers. Resolution of one thread raises another.

4. EMOTIONAL DEPTH
Does this concept have the capacity for genuine, complex feeling?

Sub-criteria:
(a) RECOGNITION: Does the target effect name a feeling the reader will recognize — something they've experienced but never seen articulated this way? Not just precision but the shock of identification: "Oh — that's what that is." ("The Dead": the specific hollowness of realizing your partner's deepest love was for someone you never knew. "Never Let Me Go": the horror of recognizing your own acceptance of what should be unacceptable.)
(b) EMOTIONAL COMPLEXITY: Does the concept support contradictory or layered feelings — where the reader feels two things at once? Name the contradiction. ("Lolita": revulsion at Humbert AND seduction by his prose. "A Good Man Is Hard to Find": the grandmother's final gesture is both the most self-deluded and the most genuine thing she's ever done. "The Ones Who Walk Away from Omelas": moral clarity AND complicity.)
(c) EMOTIONAL SOURCE: Where does the feeling come from — character, situation, concept, voice, or structural constraint? Is the source load-bearing (remove it and the emotion collapses)? ("Hills Like White Elephants": voice and omission — remove the constraint of never naming the thing and the story collapses. "Flowers for Algernon": structural constraint — the prose style IS the intelligence, so its degradation IS the feeling.)
(d) IMPLICATION: Does the concept create conditions where the reader is not merely observing but participating? Where reading mirrors something within the story? ("Lolita": enjoying the prose = participating in Humbert's aestheticization. "Omelas": continuing to read = choosing to stay. "Never Let Me Go": accepting the premise without outrage = mirroring the characters' acceptance.)

1: Emotionally flat. Names emotions without producing them. No recognition, no complexity, no implication.
5: The concept alters the reader's relationship to themselves. Emotions are specific, contradictory, and load-bearing. The reader is implicated.

5. THEMATIC RESONANCE
Does this connect to something universal that resists easy answers?

Sub-criteria:
(a) QUESTION VS MESSAGE: Does the concept pose a genuine question that resists resolution, or deliver a position? State the question as a dilemma where both sides have weight. ("The Ones Who Walk Away from Omelas": is one child's suffering an acceptable price for a city's happiness? Both answers are monstrous. "Bartleby": is his refusal radical freedom or pathological withdrawal? The story holds both.)
(b) EMBEDDEDNESS: Is the thematic concern inseparable from the premise — or could you swap in a different theme without changing the story? ("The Lottery" IS the question of inherited violence — the ritual is the theme. "The Metamorphosis" IS alienation made literal. Contrast: a detective premise can be about justice, loneliness, or nothing — the theme is bolted on.)

1: No theme, or a platitude. Has a message rather than a question. Reader wouldn't think about anything larger.
5: Reframes a universal tension so it feels new. The question is the premise and the premise is the question. Inseparable.

6. CONCEPT COHERENCE
Do the genome's elements work as a system?

Sub-criteria:
(a) LOAD-BEARING ELEMENTS: For each major genome element (premise, character seeds, constraints, style hint), would removing it make the concept generic or broken? How many are load-bearing vs decorative? (In "The Lottery," every element is load-bearing: remove the cheerful tone and it's just violence; remove the stones and there's no communal complicity; remove the ordinariness and the horror vanishes.)
(b) SURFACE/DEPTH ARCHITECTURE: Where genome elements push against each other, does the friction generate energy or confusion? The highest form is structural irony: the surface tells one story while the depth tells another, and the reader holds both. ("The Lottery": pastoral surface, horror depth. "A Modest Proposal": policy analysis surface, savage indictment depth. "Never Let Me Go": boarding school novel surface, dystopian nightmare depth.) Name the gap between what the concept appears to be and what it actually is.

1: Elements actively contradict with no productive purpose. Assembled from separate concepts.
5: A system. Every element load-bearing, every element transforms every other. Surface and depth in productive tension. Remove any piece and the concept collapses.

7. GENERATIVE FERTILITY
Does this concept suggest multiple possible stories, or only one obvious execution?

Sub-criteria:
(a) EXECUTION DIVERSITY: Can you sketch at least 3 meaningfully different stories from this premise — different in structure, tone, or emphasis, not just surface variation? ("The Metamorphosis" could be horror, dark comedy, family drama, or allegory — Kafka chose all four. "The Lottery" could be told from the winner's, the children's, or the one dissenter's perspective — each yields a fundamentally different story.)
(b) GENERATIVE PRINCIPLE VS SITUATION: Does the concept contain an engine (a mechanism that produces possibilities) or just a setup (a single scenario)? ("Library of Babel" is a generative principle — a library of every possible book produces infinite implications. "Flowers for Algernon"'s diary-as-intelligence-meter is an engine — the form produces narrative momentum as it degrades. "A woman returns to her hometown" is a situation — it describes but doesn't generate.)

1: Single execution path. The premise dictates the plot. Any writer produces the same story.
5: Inexhaustible. Contains a generative principle, not just a situation. Different writers across decades would execute completely differently.

8. SCOPE CALIBRATION
Can this produce a satisfying story in ~1,000-8,000 words?

Sub-criteria:
(a) NATURAL SIZE: Count the concept's characters, locations, time periods, and complexity layers. Does the quantity fit the word count? ("Hills Like White Elephants" — two people, one location, one conversation — is a perfect short story scope. "The Lottery" — one event, one village, one morning — is calibrated exactly.)
(b) CONSTRAINT AS COMPRESSION: Do the concept's constraints help scope (forcing economy) or hurt it (requiring setup)? ("Flowers for Algernon"'s diary format forces scene-level compression naturally. A concept requiring elaborate worldbuilding fights the short form by demanding setup before story can begin.)

1: Severe mismatch. Requires novel-length treatment or too thin for 500 words.
5: Concept and short story form made for each other. The word count constraint improves the story.

9. INDELIBILITY
Would this concept stick in a reader's mind?

Sub-criteria:
(a) INDELIBLE IMAGE — the anchor scene: Evaluate the `anchor_scene.sketch` directly. Is this scene specific, sensory, and charged enough to persist in memory? Would a reader remember it a week from now? A month? The reference standard is the scene that *became* the story in cultural memory: the black box and the stones; the grandmother reaching out to touch the Misfit; Gregor's family eating dinner while he clings to the ceiling; the blind man guiding the narrator's hand. These anchors survived because each is inescapably specific — you cannot paraphrase them into "a story about ritual violence" or "a story about an encounter."
(b) THE IRREDUCIBLE REMAINDER: After reading this premise, does something linger that the mind cannot metabolize or file away — a permanent gap, an unresolvable tension, a demand the story makes and never withdraws? Or does the concept fully discharge on first reading? ("Omelas" never stops asking its question. "Bartleby" never explains. A twist-ending thriller discharges fully on the reveal — nothing lingers.)
(c) SILHOUETTE: If you read this concept today and something reminded you of it years from now, would you recognize it? Does the concept have an irreducible shape that survives memory's compression — or would the details blur into "a story about grief" or "a story about technology"? ("The Lottery" fires every time you see a cheerful community tradition. "The Yellow Wallpaper" fires every time you hear a woman's perception dismissed. "Bartleby" fires every time someone quietly refuses to participate. These concepts became lenses.)

1: Forgettable. No image persists, no question lingers, no shape survives. Blends into the mass of competent fiction.
5: Haunting. The concept becomes a lens the reader sees the world through. The image, the question, and the shape are all indelible.
