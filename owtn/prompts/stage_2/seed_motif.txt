TASK: For this concept, produce three outputs.

1. motif_sketches — 2-3 candidate motifs, each with a three-sentence sketch of how it could move through the story. A motif is a concrete recurring element (imagery, object, phrase, character obsession) that the DAG's beats will thread through. Motifs are physical and specific, not abstract: "stones" and "the black box" are motifs; "mortality" and "tradition" are themes. Draw candidates from the premise, character seeds, setting seeds, and the anchor scene itself. Each candidate must pass four tests before you emit it:

LITERAL FUSION. The motif must already be doing literal work somewhere in the concept — handled, named, or causally active in a specific beat — before it carries any symbolic load. If the motif feels imported as a symbol rather than belonging to the scene on practical narrative grounds, discard it. The wooden leg in O'Connor's "Good Country People" works because it IS Hulga before it symbolizes anything; "the truer the symbol, the deeper it leads you."

TRANSFORMABILITY. The motif must be capable of returning with altered valence — performed by a character, made causally active, or inverted at the climax. If only the modes `introduced` and `echoed` are plausible, the motif is inert. Rhyming action that is too clunky or exact fails; the second occurrence must differ from the first in a meaning-generating way.

LOADING. The motif should be loadable from more than one thematic direction at once. The wooden leg is simultaneously disability, identity, philosophical stance, and the object of theft. A motif that can only mean one thing — even if it can be inverted — is thinner than one that carries multiple thematic vectors per appearance.

THREE-SENTENCE TEST. For each surviving candidate, write three distinct sentences (the `introduction`, `transformation`, and `climactic_action` fields of the motif_sketch): the motif's first appearance, its return with shifted valence, and a climactic firing where the motif causally drives or is causally driven by the story's central act. The three sentences must be materially different from each other and each must advance the story. Candidates that produce three sentences that collapse to the same situation in different verb tenses are inert — drop them and pick a stronger candidate.

These motifs are candidates the structure may surface, transform, or abandon — not contracts. The DAG operators choose per-node motif modes from actual beat content during MCTS expansion; your sketches are a thinking aid, not a binding plan. Pick plausible vessels, not motifs that demand specific beats.

2. concept_demands — predicates that escape the standard schema (node roles, edge types, motif modes, story_constraints). Most concepts emit ZERO demands; the schema is sufficient. Emit one only when the concept's central structural mechanism cannot be expressed by any of those primitives. Worked examples: "the DAG must include a beat that addresses the reader directly, breaking the diegetic third-person frame"; "the closing beat must mirror or invert the opening's premise as a structural rhyme". A spurious demand will rank good DAGs below worse ones at Tier 3 — favor an empty list when in doubt.

3. target_bucket — pick the natural prose-length bucket for this concept's structural ambition. The DAG that gets built will target the beat range for this bucket; under-shooting starves a rich concept of necessary setup, over-shooting forces filler. Pick the bucket that matches the concept's structural ambition, not the bucket that feels safest. Also produce `bucket_reasoning` — 2-4 sentences citing the concept signals (timeline span, character count, withholding load, thematic-engine complexity) that drove the choice.

TARGET WORD-COUNT BUCKETS:
- flash: ~1,000 words / 3-5 beats. Single-image / single-event compression. The whole story turns on one moment.
- short_short: ~3,000 words / 5-8 beats. Tight short story; single arc; restrained development. Anchor + ~2-3 setup + ~2-3 falling.
- standard_short: ~5,000 words / 7-12 beats. Fully-developed short with discernible setup-development-anchor-resolution shape. Multiple convergent threads possible.
- long_short: ~10,000 words / 10-18 beats. Long short with sustained midpoint structure, multiple POV-implied scenes, interleaved threads, room for substantial withholding architecture.

Bucket-choice signals to weight:
- TIMELINE SPAN: 'one afternoon' → flash; 'eleven months of sessions' → standard or long.
- WITHHOLDING ARCHITECTURE: stories that withhold a named-but-unstated thing need pre-anchor accumulation beats; pure incident stories don't.
- CHARACTER COUNT: 1 character + apparatus → tighter; 3+ named characters with implied arcs → larger.
- INFORMATION ARCHITECTURE: reveals that reframe earlier beats need those earlier beats to exist; climaxes that resolve action need less setup.
- THEMATIC ENGINE COMPLEXITY: multi-axis themes need multiple convergent paths; single-test concepts need fewer.
